1. Q: What is VLSI and why is it important?
   A: VLSI (Very Large Scale Integration) is the process of integrating millions or billions of transistors on a single chip. It enables modern processors, memory, and SoCs with high performance and low power.

2. Q: Differentiate between front-end and back-end VLSI design.
   A: Front-end includes specification, RTL coding, and functional verification. Back-end includes synthesis, floorplanning, placement, CTS, routing, signoff, and physical verification.

3. Q: What is Moore's Law and is it still valid?
   A: Moore's Law predicts transistor density roughly doubling every 18-24 months. It is slowing in pure scaling terms, but innovation continues through new architectures, packaging, and process technologies.

4. Q: What is a standard cell in ASIC design?
   A: A standard cell is a pre-characterized logic cell (e.g., NAND, INV, DFF) from a library with known timing, area, and power used for synthesis and P&R.

5. Q: What is RTL?
   A: RTL (Register Transfer Level) describes digital hardware behavior in terms of registers and combinational logic, typically written in Verilog or VHDL.

6. Q: What is logic synthesis?
   A: Logic synthesis converts RTL into a gate-level netlist using technology libraries while optimizing timing, area, and power constraints.

7. Q: What is a netlist?
   A: A netlist is a connectivity description of logic cells and wires representing the circuit after synthesis or extraction.

8. Q: What is timing closure?
   A: Timing closure is the iterative process of meeting setup/hold requirements across all paths and corners by optimizing logic, placement, routing, and constraints.

9. Q: Explain setup time.
   A: Setup time is the minimum interval before the active clock edge during which data must remain stable at a flip-flop input.

10. Q: Explain hold time.
    A: Hold time is the minimum interval after the active clock edge during which data must remain stable at a flip-flop input.

11. Q: What causes setup violations?
    A: Slow data path, fast clock path to capture flop, high fanout, poor placement, excessive logic depth, or pessimistic constraints can cause setup violations.

12. Q: What causes hold violations?
    A: Very fast data paths, clock skew, low delay between flops, and process variations can cause hold violations.

13. Q: What is clock skew?
    A: Clock skew is the arrival-time difference of the same clock edge at different sequential elements.

14. Q: What is clock jitter?
    A: Jitter is short-term variation in clock edge timing from its ideal periodic position.

15. Q: What is CTS?
    A: Clock Tree Synthesis builds the clock distribution network to balance skew, control latency, and meet timing and power goals.

16. Q: What is STA?
    A: Static Timing Analysis checks timing without simulation by analyzing path delays under constraints across process-voltage-temperature corners.

17. Q: What is MCMM analysis?
    A: MCMM (Multi-Corner Multi-Mode) analyzes timing and constraints over multiple operating modes and PVT corners to ensure robust silicon behavior.

18. Q: What is OCV?
    A: On-Chip Variation models local/global delay variation due to manufacturing and environmental differences across a die.

19. Q: What is AOCV/POCV?
    A: AOCV and POCV are advanced derating methods to model path-depth-dependent and statistical timing variation more accurately than fixed derates.

20. Q: What is a false path?
    A: A false path is a logically impossible path for data capture in normal operation and should be excluded from timing checks.

21. Q: What is a multicycle path?
    A: A multicycle path is allowed more than one clock cycle for data propagation, defined using proper setup and hold multicycle constraints.

22. Q: What is crosstalk in VLSI?
    A: Crosstalk is noise coupling between adjacent interconnects, causing delay shifts or glitches that can impact timing and signal integrity.

23. Q: What is IR drop?
    A: IR drop is voltage loss in the power grid due to resistance and current flow, reducing cell drive strength and potentially causing timing failures.

24. Q: What is electromigration?
    A: Electromigration is metal degradation due to high current density, causing voids/hillocks and long-term reliability failures.

25. Q: What is dynamic power in CMOS?
    A: Dynamic power mainly comes from charging/discharging capacitances and is approximately P = alpha * C * V^2 * f.

26. Q: What is leakage power?
    A: Leakage power is static power consumed even when transistors are not switching, dominated by subthreshold and gate leakage currents.

27. Q: How do you reduce dynamic power?
    A: Use clock gating, lower voltage/frequency, reduce switching activity, optimize capacitance, and use multi-bit flops where appropriate.

28. Q: How do you reduce leakage power?
    A: Use high-Vt cells on non-critical paths, power gating, body bias techniques, and process-aware cell selection.

29. Q: What is floorplanning?
    A: Floorplanning decides die/core size, macro locations, power structure, and block arrangement to optimize routability and timing.

30. Q: What is placement?
    A: Placement positions standard cells in legal rows to optimize timing, wirelength, congestion, and power.

31. Q: What is routing?
    A: Routing creates physical interconnections between pins using metal layers while honoring design rules and timing constraints.

32. Q: What are DRC and LVS?
    A: DRC checks layout against manufacturing rules; LVS checks whether the extracted layout netlist matches the schematic/netlist connectivity.

33. Q: What is parasitic extraction?
    A: Extraction computes RC parasitics from layout interconnects/devices for accurate post-route timing and signal integrity analysis.

34. Q: What is a PVT corner?
    A: A PVT corner is a combination of process, voltage, and temperature conditions used to model best/worst silicon behavior.

35. Q: What is useful skew?
    A: Useful skew intentionally adjusts clock arrival times to improve setup slack while still satisfying hold constraints.

36. Q: What is scan chain and why is it used?
    A: Scan chain links flip-flops into shift registers for controllability/observability during manufacturing test and ATPG pattern application.

37. Q: What is ATPG?
    A: Automatic Test Pattern Generation creates test vectors to detect modeled defects (e.g., stuck-at, transition faults) in scan-based designs.

38. Q: What is equivalence checking?
    A: Equivalence checking formally verifies that two design representations (e.g., RTL vs synthesized netlist) are functionally identical.

39. Q: What is CDC and why important?
    A: Clock Domain Crossing concerns signals crossing asynchronous clocks; poor CDC design can cause metastability and intermittent failures.

40. Q: How do you handle metastability?
    A: Use synchronizers (e.g., 2-flop sync), proper CDC protocols (handshake/FIFO), and avoid combinational logic between synchronizer stages.

41. Q: What is reset synchronizer?
    A: It safely deasserts asynchronous reset in each clock domain to prevent metastability and reset-release hazards.

42. Q: What is UPF in low-power design?
    A: UPF (Unified Power Format) specifies power intent including domains, isolation, retention, and power state behavior.

43. Q: What is isolation cell?
    A: An isolation cell clamps signals from a powered-down domain to prevent unknown values from corrupting active domains.

44. Q: What is retention flop?
    A: A retention flop preserves key state through power-down using a retention supply, enabling faster restore on wake-up.

45. Q: What is ECO in VLSI?
    A: Engineering Change Order is a late-stage design modification for bug fixes or timing closure with minimal layout disruption.

46. Q: What is congestion and how do you fix it?
    A: Congestion is over-utilized routing regions. Fixes include placement spreading, floorplan changes, buffering strategy updates, and layer assignment optimization.

47. Q: What is the difference between FPGA and ASIC?
    A: FPGA is programmable and fast to prototype but slower and less power-efficient; ASIC is fixed-function with better PPA at high NRE cost and longer development.

48. Q: What is signoff in physical design?
    A: Signoff is final verification for timing, power integrity, SI, DRC/LVS, EM/IR, and reliability before tape-out.

49. Q: What is tape-out?
    A: Tape-out is the final release of verified layout data (e.g., GDSII/OASIS) to the foundry for fabrication.

50. Q: What metrics are used to evaluate VLSI implementation quality?
    A: Common metrics are PPA (performance, power, area), timing slack/WNS/TNS, DRC/LVS clean status, EM/IR margins, yield, and test coverage.
